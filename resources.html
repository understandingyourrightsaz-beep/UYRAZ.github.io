Police Encounters

Your constitutional rights during stops, searches, arrests, and interrogations. Select your status to expand.

Key Constitutional Rights

The 4th Amendment requires police to have probable cause before searching your belongings or taking you into custody. The 5th Amendment ensures you are not required to provide evidence against yourself. The 6th Amendment gives you the right to a lawyer — if you cannot afford one, the court must provide one. (GIDEON V. WAINWRIGHT) The 14th Amendment forces state and local police to respect these protections. You do not need to sign any documents. You may ask to speak to a lawyer at any time.

Reasonable Suspicion vs. Probable Cause

Reasonable Suspicion — specific, articulable facts suggesting criminal activity — justifies a brief investigative (Terry) stop. (TERRY V. OHIO) Probable Cause — facts a reasonable person would use to believe a crime was committed — is required for arrest and most warrants. (ILLINOIS V. GATES)

Stops, Frisks, Detentions & Arrests

Police may briefly detain you with reasonable suspicion and perform a pat-down for weapons if they reasonably believe you are armed. (TERRY V. OHIO) Arrests require: an arrest warrant, witnessing you commit a crime, or probable cause of a felony. (BECK V. OHIO) In all encounters you retain: the right to remain silent, the right to an attorney, the right to a phone call, and protection from unreasonable searches.

Warrants

Search Warrant: Signed by a judge, must describe the exact place and items sought, supported by probable cause. (FRANKS V. DELAWARE) Arrest Warrant: Based on probable cause, issued by a neutral judge, usually required to arrest inside the home absent emergency. (PAYTON V. NEW YORK) If no valid warrant, you can refuse consent and the officer may not enter your home.

Warrant Exceptions

Consent — voluntary agreement removes the need for a warrant. You have the right to refuse. (SCHNECKLOTH V. BUSTAMONTE) | Search incident to arrest (CHIMEL V. CALIFORNIA) | Plain view (HORTON V. CALIFORNIA) | Exigent circumstances (KENTUCKY V. KING) | Vehicle searches with probable cause (CARROLL V. U.S.) | Inventory searches of impounded vehicles (SOUTH DAKOTA V. OPPERMAN)

Miranda Rights & Interrogation

Before a custodial interrogation, police must tell you: (1) you have the right to remain silent, (2) anything you say can be used against you, (3) you have the right to an attorney, (4) if you cannot afford one, one will be provided. (MIRANDA V. ARIZONA) Clearly invoke your right to silence or request a lawyer — questioning must stop. (EDWARDS V. ARIZONA)

Exclusionary Rule & Digital Evidence

Evidence from Fourth Amendment violations is generally inadmissible. (MAPP V. OHIO) "Fruit of the poisonous tree" also blocks derivative evidence. (WONG SUN V. U.S.) Police need a warrant to search your cell phone after arrest (RILEY V. CALIFORNIA) and for long-term cell-site location records. (CARPENTER V. U.S.) You have the First Amendment right to record police in public. (GLIK V. CUNNIFFE)

Same Core Rights

Green card holders have the same constitutional protections as citizens for searches (4th), self-incrimination and due process (5th), criminal-trial rights including counsel (6th), and incorporation against states (14th). You may ask to speak to a lawyer. Important: You MUST carry your green card at all times and present it when asked. (8 USC § 1304(e))

Key Differences

Criminal + Immigration Consequences: Criminal outcomes can produce separate civil immigration penalties — removal, inadmissibility — in addition to criminal penalties. (8 U.S.C. § 1227)

No free lawyer in immigration court. The government does not appoint counsel in civil immigration cases. Your criminal defense attorney must advise you of deportation risks. (PADILLA V. KENTUCKY)

Arrests trigger immigration consequences. Even minor arrests can lead to ICE detainers and removal proceedings. (8 C.F.R. § 287.7)

Borders

At borders, green card holders are subject to admissibility determinations upon return — border searches can lead to removal proceedings. Miranda and the 5th Amendment operate the same way in criminal custody, but statements can affect both criminal charges and immigration status.

Constitutional Rights You Have

Regardless of status, you have: freedom from unreasonable searches and seizures (4th Amendment), the right not to testify against yourself (5th Amendment), the right to a lawyer if charged with a crime punishable by imprisonment (6th Amendment), and these protections apply in every state (14th Amendment). Your criminal defense attorney must advise you about how a plea could affect your immigration status. (PADILLA V. KENTUCKY)

Key Differences

No free lawyer in immigration court. You must hire a private attorney or find free legal aid. (8 U.S.C. § 1229a)

Even minor criminal arrests trigger immigration consequences. (8 U.S.C. § 1227)

Booking data shared with ICE. Fingerprints and identity data from jail booking are often shared with ICE. (8 C.F.R. § 287.7)

Suppression rules differ in immigration court. Evidence excluded from criminal trial may still be used in immigration hearings. (I.N.S. V. LOPEZ-MENDOZA)

Stops, Home Entry & Miranda

Police must still have reasonable suspicion for a stop and probable cause or a warrant for an arrest. Being undocumented is NOT by itself a federal crime. ICE administrative warrants (I-200) do NOT authorize forced home entry — ask to see any warrant and verify it is signed by a judge.

Article 36 — Vienna Convention

If you are a foreign national arrested or detained, authorities must inform you that you can have your consulate notified. If you request it, the consulate must be notified. They can help contact your family, provide attorney lists, and visit you in custody. (VIENNA CONVENTION ART. 36)

ICE & Immigration

Your rights during encounters with Immigration and Customs Enforcement — including how to identify a valid warrant, limits on ICE authority, and home entry rules.

ICE Authority & Your Rights

ICE's civil immigration enforcement targets noncitizens — removal does not apply to U.S. citizens. ICE may not lawfully arrest, hold, or remove a U.S. citizen for immigration purposes. (ICE DIRECTIVE 16001.2) You retain the right to remain silent and to refuse entry or consent to searches if a valid judicial warrant is not presented.

How to Identify a Valid Warrant
  • Signed by a judge or magistrate — not an ICE officer or DHS official
  • Lists the name of a court at the top (federal or state court)
  • Search warrant: lists the exact address and specific items/areas to be searched
  • Arrest warrant: lists the full name of the person to be arrested
  • Is current, dated, and not expired
